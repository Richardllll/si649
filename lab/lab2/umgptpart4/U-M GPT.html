Do you know the famous Napoleon's March figure by Minard?

Yes, I am familiar with Charles Minard's famous cartographic depiction known as "Napoleon's March." The chart is a flow map that illustrates the disastrous Russian campaign of 1812, showing the size of Napoleon’s army as it advanced to and retreated from Moscow. Created in 1869, it is considered a pioneering piece of information visualization.

The map uses various visual elements to convey data:

  • The thickness of a wide colored band represents the size of the army at various geographical points as they moved through Europe toward Moscow and then back.
  • The band narrows dramatically, illustrating the devastating losses suffered due to battles, harsh weather, disease, and supply shortages.
  • On the return journey, a temperature graph at the bottom captures the freezing conditions faced by the army, contributing to the high mortality.

Minard's work is renowned for effectively integrating data about location, direction, time, and temperature into a single, comprehensible narrative representation, allowing viewers to grasp both the scale of the disaster and the conditions that contributed to it. It's often cited in discussions about excellence in information design.
